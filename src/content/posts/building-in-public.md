## The fear of shipping incomplete work

For years, I waited until things felt "ready" before showing anyone. The result was a graveyard of half-finished projects that never saw daylight.

Building in public changed that. Not because it is trendy, but because it forces accountability. When you commit to sharing progress, you actually make progress.

## What I learned

**Start before you are ready.** The first version of anything will be embarrassing. That is the point. If you are not slightly embarrassed by your first release, you waited too long.

**Constraints are liberating.** Working in public means you cannot endlessly iterate. You ship, you get feedback, you iterate in the open. The deadline is self-imposed and non-negotiable.

**Community is a multiplier.** The best insights come from people who are not you. A stranger on GitHub will find a bug in five minutes that you overlooked for weeks.

## The practical side

I started with small things: dotfiles, configuration snippets, a utility function. Nothing groundbreaking. But each small share built confidence and attracted people who cared about similar problems.

The key is consistency. Not daily tweets about productivity. Real work, shared honestly. Code commits. Design decisions. Trade-offs explained.

## What I would do differently

I would have started sooner. I would have shared more of the ugly parts—the failed experiments, the dead ends, the decisions that did not work out. Those are more valuable than the polished final product.

Building in public is not about marketing. It is about learning in the open and letting others learn from your mistakes.
