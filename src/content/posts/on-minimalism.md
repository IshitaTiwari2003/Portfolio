## The temptation of features

Every product starts simple. Then the feature requests come. Each one seems reasonable in isolation. Together, they create complexity that suffocates the original vision.

Minimalism in software is not about removing things. It is about being intentional about what stays.

## The cost of complexity

Every feature has a cost that extends beyond development time:

- **Cognitive load** — Users must understand what the feature does and when to use it
- **Maintenance burden** — Code that exists must be maintained, tested, and updated
- **Interaction complexity** — Features do not exist in isolation; they affect each other

The real cost of a feature is not the day you build it. It is every day after that.

## Designing with restraint

The best interfaces feel inevitable. They do not explain themselves because they do not need to. Every element earns its place.

I think of it as editing, not designing. Start with everything you think you need, then remove things until you cannot remove anything more without losing meaning.

**The test:** If you can remove a feature and users do not notice, it should not exist.

## Practical minimalism

This does not mean building bare-boned products. It means being thoughtful about scope:

- **Start with one use case.** Solve it perfectly before expanding.
- **Default to fewer options.** Most settings are a sign of indecision.
- **Let the interface breathe.** White space is not empty space; it is structure.

The goal is not to make something small. The goal is to make something clear.

## The hard part

Saying no to features is easier than saying no to users. Every request comes from someone who genuinely needs something. The discipline is remembering that you are building for everyone, not just the loudest voices.

Minimalism is a choice you make every day. It is not a one-time decision but an ongoing practice of editing, refining, and sometimes letting go.
